Unit 1

Introduction Unit 1 introduced the core principles of Security and Risk Management and established the conceptual foundation for the rest of the module. The unit focused on defining risk, understanding how risks are assessed and mitigated, and comparing different approaches to risk evaluation. It also introduced common standards and structured risk management processes, which are central to security decision-making in organisations. The material in this unit aligns well with established guidance that treats risk management as a structured process of identifying, assessing, and reducing risk to an acceptable level. Artefact Overview The artefacts for this unit include my lecture and reading notes, my participation in the first collaborative discussion, my early contribution to team formation and the group contract, and my initial engagement with the module wiki and seminar preparation. These artefacts show that I did not only engage with the theory, but also began applying it in a collaborative academic setting. This is consistent with the module brief, which expects the e-portfolio to include artefacts demonstrating development across the module, individual contributions to projects, and structured reflective content. Key Concepts Learned One of the most important ideas in this unit was that risk is not simply “danger,” but something that must be analysed in relation to both likelihood and impact. NIST’s glossary describes risk as the net negative impact arising from the exercise of a vulnerability, taking into account both probability and impact, which closely matches the way the unit introduced risk in a security management context. Another key concept was the distinction between threats and vulnerabilities. This distinction is essential because risk cannot be discussed meaningfully without considering both what could cause harm and what weakness might be exploited. I learned that risk assessment is stronger when these elements are separated clearly, because that makes it easier to determine what needs to be protected, where weaknesses exist, and what type of mitigation is appropriate. NIST defines risk assessment as a process that includes threat and vulnerability analysis while also considering mitigating controls already planned or in place. The unit also introduced qualitative and quantitative approaches to risk assessment. From my understanding, qualitative assessment is useful when organisations need to classify risks using categories such as low, medium, or high, especially when exact numerical data is limited. Quantitative assessment, by contrast, attempts to express risk using measurable values, which can support more detailed comparison and prioritisation. Learning about both methods helped me understand that risk management is not a one-size-fits-all activity; the method chosen depends on the context, the availability of data, and the purpose of the analysis. A further important area was the risk management process itself. ISO explains risk management as a process that can be used to identify, analyse, evaluate, treat, monitor, and communicate risk, while NIST describes risk management as identifying risk, assessing risk, and taking steps to reduce it to an acceptable level. Together, these sources closely reflect the process-oriented view presented in Unit 1. Finally, the unit introduced common standards and frameworks used in security and risk management. ISO 31000 provides general guidelines for managing risk across organisations, while NIST SP 800-37 Rev. 2 provides a structured Risk Management Framework for information systems and organisations. This helped me understand that standards are not abstract theory; they are practical tools that support consistency, accountability, and informed security decisions. Skills Developed This unit helped me develop several important academic and professional skills. First, it improved my ability to think analytically about risk by separating concepts that are often confused in everyday language, such as threats, vulnerabilities, impact, and likelihood. Second, it improved my understanding of how structured frameworks support security management in practice. Third, it strengthened my ability to compare approaches rather than accept a single model uncritically. Finally, through discussion and team-related activities, I began developing the collaborative skills needed to contribute to group-based security work, which is particularly important because risk management in practice is rarely an individual task. Critical Reflection This unit changed the way I think about security. Before studying it, I tended to think about security problems mainly in technical terms, focusing on threats or attacks themselves. After engaging with the unit, I now see that effective security management depends just as much on structured analysis, prioritisation, and decision-making as it does on technical controls. In other words, security is not only about stopping attacks, but about understanding which risks matter most, why they matter, and what level of mitigation is realistic. One of the most useful things I learned was that different risk assessment approaches serve different purposes. At first, qualitative assessment seemed too simple to be valuable. However, I came to understand that it can be very effective when speed, communication, or limited data make numerical modelling impractical. At the same time, I also recognised the benefit of quantitative approaches where stronger evidence and more detailed prioritisation are needed. This made me more aware of the importance of selecting methods that fit the organisational situation rather than assuming one method is always superior. I also found the introduction to standards and frameworks particularly valuable. Seeing that ISO 31000 and NIST RMF both present risk management as a disciplined, repeatable process helped me connect classroom learning to professional practice. This gave me greater confidence that the concepts introduced in Unit 1 are not merely academic, but directly relevant to how modern organisations manage security and operational risk. In addition, the collaborative elements of the week made me reflect on the human side of risk management. Security decisions are shaped not only by technical facts, but also by communication, shared understanding, and team coordination. My involvement in the early team activities showed me that even at the beginning of a project, clear communication and defined responsibilities matter. This is something I expect to carry forward into later units and future professional work. Conclusion Overall, Unit 1 provided a strong introduction to Security and Risk Management by establishing the core language, concepts, and frameworks that support the discipline. It helped me understand risk as a structured and manageable issue rather than a vague security concern. The unit also improved my ability to think critically about assessment methods, standards, and practical decision-making. As a result, this unit created a solid basis for later study in identifying, analysing, and mitigating security risks in a more informed and professional way.
