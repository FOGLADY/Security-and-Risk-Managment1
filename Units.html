Unit 1

Unit 1 introduced the core principles of Security and Risk Management and established the conceptual foundation for the rest of the module. The unit focused on defining risk, understanding how risks are assessed and mitigated, and comparing different approaches to risk evaluation. It also introduced common standards and structured risk management processes, which are central to security decision-making in organisations. The material in this unit aligns well with established guidance that treats risk management as a structured process of identifying, assessing, and reducing risk to an acceptable level. Artefact Overview The artefacts for this unit include my lecture and reading notes, my participation in the first collaborative discussion, my early contribution to team formation and the group contract, and my initial engagement with the module wiki and seminar preparation. These artefacts show that I did not only engage with the theory, but also began applying it in a collaborative academic setting. This is consistent with the module brief, which expects the e-portfolio to include artefacts demonstrating development across the module, individual contributions to projects, and structured reflective content. Key Concepts Learned One of the most important ideas in this unit was that risk is not simply “danger,” but something that must be analysed in relation to both likelihood and impact. NIST’s glossary describes risk as the net negative impact arising from the exercise of a vulnerability, taking into account both probability and impact, which closely matches the way the unit introduced risk in a security management context. Another key concept was the distinction between threats and vulnerabilities. This distinction is essential because risk cannot be discussed meaningfully without considering both what could cause harm and what weakness might be exploited. I learned that risk assessment is stronger when these elements are separated clearly, because that makes it easier to determine what needs to be protected, where weaknesses exist, and what type of mitigation is appropriate. NIST defines risk assessment as a process that includes threat and vulnerability analysis while also considering mitigating controls already planned or in place. The unit also introduced qualitative and quantitative approaches to risk assessment. From my understanding, qualitative assessment is useful when organisations need to classify risks using categories such as low, medium, or high, especially when exact numerical data is limited. Quantitative assessment, by contrast, attempts to express risk using measurable values, which can support more detailed comparison and prioritisation. Learning about both methods helped me understand that risk management is not a one-size-fits-all activity; the method chosen depends on the context, the availability of data, and the purpose of the analysis. A further important area was the risk management process itself. ISO explains risk management as a process that can be used to identify, analyse, evaluate, treat, monitor, and communicate risk, while NIST describes risk management as identifying risk, assessing risk, and taking steps to reduce it to an acceptable level. Together, these sources closely reflect the process-oriented view presented in Unit 1. Finally, the unit introduced common standards and frameworks used in security and risk management. ISO 31000 provides general guidelines for managing risk across organisations, while NIST SP 800-37 Rev. 2 provides a structured Risk Management Framework for information systems and organisations. This helped me understand that standards are not abstract theory; they are practical tools that support consistency, accountability, and informed security decisions. Skills Developed This unit helped me develop several important academic and professional skills. First, it improved my ability to think analytically about risk by separating concepts that are often confused in everyday language, such as threats, vulnerabilities, impact, and likelihood. Second, it improved my understanding of how structured frameworks support security management in practice. Third, it strengthened my ability to compare approaches rather than accept a single model uncritically. Finally, through discussion and team-related activities, I began developing the collaborative skills needed to contribute to group-based security work, which is particularly important because risk management in practice is rarely an individual task. Critical Reflection This unit changed the way I think about security. Before studying it, I tended to think about security problems mainly in technical terms, focusing on threats or attacks themselves. After engaging with the unit, I now see that effective security management depends just as much on structured analysis, prioritisation, and decision-making as it does on technical controls. In other words, security is not only about stopping attacks, but about understanding which risks matter most, why they matter, and what level of mitigation is realistic. One of the most useful things I learned was that different risk assessment approaches serve different purposes. At first, qualitative assessment seemed too simple to be valuable. However, I came to understand that it can be very effective when speed, communication, or limited data make numerical modelling impractical. At the same time, I also recognised the benefit of quantitative approaches where stronger evidence and more detailed prioritisation are needed. This made me more aware of the importance of selecting methods that fit the organisational situation rather than assuming one method is always superior. I also found the introduction to standards and frameworks particularly valuable. Seeing that ISO 31000 and NIST RMF both present risk management as a disciplined, repeatable process helped me connect classroom learning to professional practice. This gave me greater confidence that the concepts introduced in Unit 1 are not merely academic, but directly relevant to how modern organisations manage security and operational risk. In addition, the collaborative elements of the week made me reflect on the human side of risk management. Security decisions are shaped not only by technical facts, but also by communication, shared understanding, and team coordination. My involvement in the early team activities showed me that even at the beginning of a project, clear communication and defined responsibilities matter. This is something I expect to carry forward into later units and future professional work. Conclusion Overall, Unit 1 provided a strong introduction to Security and Risk Management by establishing the core language, concepts, and frameworks that support the discipline. It helped me understand risk as a structured and manageable issue rather than a vague security concern. The unit also improved my ability to think critically about assessment methods, standards, and practical decision-making. As a result, this unit created a solid basis for later study in identifying, analysing, and mitigating security risks in a more informed and professional way. Unit 2

Unit 2 expanded on the foundational knowledge introduced in Unit 1 by exploring the Risk Management Process (RMP) in greater depth. The focus of this unit was on comparing qualitative and quantitative risk assessment approaches and analysing the critical role of user participation within the process. Understanding these elements is essential, as risk management is not only a technical activity but also a collaborative and context-dependent process influenced by human factors. Artefact Overview The artefacts produced in this unit include my continued participation in Collaborative Discussion 1, responses to peers’ contributions, notes from the reading materials on assessment approaches and user involvement, and engagement with seminar preparation activities. These artefacts demonstrate both analytical thinking and active participation in collaborative learning, which aligns with the module’s emphasis on teamwork and continuous development. Key Concepts and Knowledge Gained A major focus of this unit was the deeper understanding of the Risk Management Process (RMP). Building on the previous unit, I reinforced the idea that risk management is not static but iterative, requiring continuous reassessment as conditions change. This aligns with established frameworks such as the NIST Risk Management Framework, which emphasises ongoing monitoring and adaptation. Another important concept explored was the comparison between qualitative and quantitative risk assessment methods. Qualitative assessment relies on subjective judgement and categorisation, making it useful in situations where time or data is limited. In contrast, quantitative assessment attempts to assign numerical values to risk, allowing for more precise analysis and comparison. Through this unit, I developed a clearer understanding that neither approach is inherently superior; rather, their effectiveness depends on the organisational context, available data, and decision-making requirements. A key addition in this unit was the role of user participation in the risk management process. I learned that users are not passive elements in security systems but active contributors to both risk and mitigation. Their behaviour, awareness, and engagement directly influence how effectively risks are identified and managed. Without user involvement, risk assessments may overlook real-world usage patterns, leading to incomplete or ineffective mitigation strategies. The unit also addressed the implications of mitigation decisions, highlighting that every control introduced can have operational, financial, or usability consequences. This reinforced the idea that risk management is about balancing security with practicality, rather than eliminating risk entirely. Skills Developed This unit strengthened several key skills: The ability to critically compare different risk assessment methodologies Understanding how human factors influence security outcomes Evaluating the trade-offs associated with mitigation strategies Applying theoretical frameworks to collaborative discussions Developing structured arguments in peer-based learning environments These skills are particularly relevant in cybersecurity roles, where decisions must often balance technical, organisational, and human considerations. Critical Reflection This unit significantly shifted my perspective on risk management by emphasising the importance of human involvement. Previously, I focused primarily on technical aspects of risk, such as vulnerabilities and threats. However, I now recognise that user behaviour can either strengthen or weaken security controls, making user participation a central element of effective risk management. One key insight was that risk assessments conducted without user input can be incomplete. For example, users may interact with systems in ways that were not originally anticipated, creating new risks that purely technical analysis might miss. This highlighted the importance of incorporating user feedback and behavioural insights into the risk management process. Additionally, comparing qualitative and quantitative approaches helped me understand that decision-making in security is often constrained by practical limitations. While quantitative methods provide more precise results, they are not always feasible due to lack of data or time. This reinforced the idea that flexibility and context-awareness are essential when choosing an assessment approach. The collaborative discussions also contributed to my learning. Engaging with peers exposed me to different perspectives and interpretations, which challenged my assumptions and improved my critical thinking. It became clear that risk management benefits from multiple viewpoints, as different individuals may identify different risks or propose alternative mitigation strategies. Conclusion Unit 2 deepened my understanding of the Risk Management Process by highlighting the importance of assessment methods and user involvement. It demonstrated that effective risk management requires not only technical knowledge but also an understanding of human behaviour and organisational context. The knowledge gained in this unit builds directly on Unit 1 and provides a more practical and realistic view of how risks are assessed and managed in real-world environments. Unit 3

Unit 3 built upon the foundational concepts of risk management by introducing structured approaches to threat modelling. While previous units focused on identifying and assessing risks, this unit shifted attention towards understanding how threats are analysed, categorised, and prioritised using formal frameworks. The unit emphasised that effective security management requires not only identifying risks, but also understanding how potential attacks may occur and how systems can be systematically evaluated against them. Artefact Overview The artefacts for this unit include my continued participation in Collaborative Discussion 1, including a final summary post integrating feedback and learning from Units 1–3, contributions to the module wiki with key terminology and concepts, and structured notes on threat modelling frameworks such as STRIDE, DREAD, Attack Trees, and PASTA. These artefacts demonstrate both theoretical understanding and the ability to synthesise information across multiple units, which aligns with the module’s expectations for continuous development and reflection. Key Concepts and Knowledge Gained A key starting point in this unit was reinforcing the distinction between threats and vulnerabilities. While vulnerabilities represent weaknesses within a system, threats are potential actions or events that can exploit those weaknesses. This distinction is essential for effective threat modelling, as it allows for a structured analysis of how systems may be attacked. The unit introduced several widely used threat modelling frameworks, including: STRIDE, which categorises threats into Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information Disclosure, Denial of Service, and Elevation of Privilege DREAD, used to evaluate and prioritise risks based on factors such as damage potential and exploitability Attack Trees, which visually represent how an attacker might achieve a specific objective PASTA, a more comprehensive and risk-driven modelling approach Through studying these models, I learned that each framework serves a different purpose. For example, STRIDE is useful for systematically identifying threats during system design, while Attack Trees are more effective for visualising attack paths. PASTA, on the other hand, provides a broader, business-oriented perspective by linking technical threats to organisational risk. Another important concept was the idea of hybrid threat modelling, where multiple techniques are combined to provide a more comprehensive analysis. This reflects real-world practice, where relying on a single model may not capture all relevant risks. The unit also introduced the use of guides and “cookbooks”, which help practitioners select appropriate frameworks depending on the context. This reinforced the idea that security is not about memorising models, but about selecting and applying them appropriately. Skills Developed This unit contributed to the development of several important skills: The ability to analyse and differentiate between threat modelling frameworks Understanding how to apply different models depending on context Visualising potential attack scenarios using structured approaches Evaluating the strengths and limitations of different techniques Synthesising knowledge across multiple units into a cohesive understanding These skills are particularly valuable in cybersecurity roles where structured threat analysis is essential for system design and defence. Critical Reflection This unit significantly deepened my understanding of how security risks are analysed in practice. In previous units, I focused primarily on identifying risks and assessing their impact. However, this unit introduced a more structured and proactive approach by showing how threats can be modelled before they occur. One key insight was that different threat modelling techniques provide different perspectives. Initially, I assumed that one framework might be sufficient for analysing threats. However, I now understand that combining approaches can lead to a more complete understanding of potential risks. For example, using STRIDE to identify threats and Attack Trees to visualise them provides both categorisation and structure. Another important realisation was the connection between technical analysis and organisational context. Frameworks such as PASTA highlight that threat modelling is not purely technical, but also involves understanding business impact and risk priorities. This aligns closely with earlier units, where risk was defined in terms of both likelihood and impact. The process of writing a summary post and engaging with peer feedback also helped reinforce my learning. By integrating ideas from multiple units and perspectives, I developed a more coherent understanding of how risk management and threat modelling fit together. This demonstrated the value of collaborative learning in refining complex concepts. Conclusion Unit 3 expanded my understanding of security by introducing structured methods for analysing threats and modelling potential attacks. It demonstrated that effective security management requires not only identifying risks but also understanding how those risks may materialise. By learning how to apply and combine different threat modelling techniques, I developed a more practical and comprehensive approach to cybersecurity analysis. Unit 7

nit 7 introduced the principles of Quantitative Risk Modelling (QRM), expanding the previous qualitative approaches by incorporating numerical and probabilistic analysis. While earlier units focused on identifying and assessing risks using descriptive methods, this unit emphasised the importance of data-driven decision-making. The unit explored several quantitative techniques, including probabilistic models such as Monte Carlo simulations and Bayesian approaches, as well as multi-criteria decision-making methods. Artefact Overview The artefacts for this unit include my engagement with risk modelling activities, participation in Collaborative Discussion 2, contributions to the module wiki, and structured notes on quantitative modelling techniques. These artefacts demonstrate the transition from conceptual and qualitative analysis toward data-driven modelling approaches, aligning with the module requirement to evidence continuous development and application of knowledge. Key Concepts and Knowledge Gained The central concept in this unit was the definition of quantitative risk modelling, which involves using numerical data and probabilistic methods to estimate risk more precisely. Unlike qualitative approaches, which rely on subjective judgement, quantitative methods aim to produce measurable and comparable outputs. One of the primary techniques introduced was Monte Carlo simulation, which uses repeated random sampling to simulate a range of possible outcomes. This approach is particularly useful when dealing with uncertainty, as it allows for the estimation of risk distributions rather than single-point values. Another key concept was the application of Bayes' theorem, which enables the updating of probabilities based on new information. This method is especially valuable in dynamic environments where risk conditions change over time, allowing for more adaptive and responsive risk assessments. The unit also introduced multi-criteria decision-making (MCDM) techniques, including: TOPSIS (Technique for Order of Preference by Similarity to Ideal Solution) Analytic Hierarchy Process Analytic Network Process These approaches are used when decisions must consider multiple factors simultaneously, such as cost, risk, and performance. An important part of the unit was understanding the advantages and limitations of each approach. Quantitative methods provide more precise and objective results, but they require reliable data and can be complex to implement. This highlighted the importance of selecting the appropriate technique based on the specific problem and available resources. Skills Developed This unit contributed to the development of several advanced analytical skills: Understanding probabilistic and statistical approaches to risk Applying quantitative methods to model uncertainty Comparing different modelling techniques based on context Evaluating strengths and limitations of complex models Integrating quantitative analysis into broader risk management strategies These skills are essential for advanced cybersecurity and risk analysis roles, where decision-making increasingly relies on data and modelling. Critical Reflection This unit significantly changed how I approach risk analysis. In earlier units, I relied primarily on qualitative assessments, which, while useful, often lacked precision. Through this unit, I realised that quantitative methods provide a deeper and more structured understanding of risk by incorporating probability and variability. One of the most important insights was the value of Monte Carlo simulations in handling uncertainty. Instead of relying on a single estimate, this method allows for the exploration of multiple possible outcomes, which provides a more realistic view of risk. This approach is particularly relevant in complex systems where outcomes are not deterministic. The introduction of Bayesian methods also highlighted the importance of adaptability in risk management. Being able to update risk assessments based on new information is critical in dynamic environments, especially in cybersecurity where threats evolve rapidly. However, I also recognised the limitations of quantitative methods. They require accurate data and can be difficult to implement without the necessary expertise. This reinforced the idea that quantitative approaches should complement, rather than replace, qualitative methods. The collaborative discussions and modelling activities helped reinforce these concepts by allowing me to compare different approaches and understand their practical implications. This strengthened my ability to critically evaluate modelling techniques rather than applying them without consideration. Conclusion Unit 7 provided a significant advancement in my understanding of risk management by introducing quantitative modelling techniques. It demonstrated how data-driven approaches can enhance the accuracy and reliability of risk assessments, while also highlighting the importance of selecting appropriate methods based on context. This unit builds on previous knowledge and prepares for more advanced applications of risk modelling in real-world scenarios. Key Concepts and Knowledge Gained A central focus of this unit was the application of quantitative risk modelling to real-world problems. Key Concepts and Knowledge Gained A central focus of this unit was the relationship between RTO/RPO requirements and disaster recovery solutions. This creates a direct link between business priorities and technical design. The unit explored several standby system configurations, including: Cold standby (low cost, slow recovery) Warm standby (moderate cost and recovery time) Hot standby (high cost, near-instant recovery) These configurations illustrate how organisations must balance performance, cost, and risk tolerance when designing DR solutions. Another important concept was Disaster Recovery as a Service (DRaaS), where cloud providers offer recovery capabilities. DRaaS can improve scalability and reduce infrastructure costs, but it also introduces challenges such as: Vendor lock-in, where organisations become dependent on a specific provider Network dependency, which can affect recovery performance Security concerns, particularly around data protection and access control The unit also emphasised the limitations of DR solutions. No solution is perfect, and all designs involve trade-offs. For example, highly resilient systems may be expensive and complex to maintain, while simpler systems may not meet strict recovery requirements.
